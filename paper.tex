\section{Introduction}
\label{section:intro}
The manual, time-consuming review of planning proposals introduces substantial delays, with cascading negative impacts on a city’s economic resilience, social equity, and long-term adaptability \cite{weinstein2024UKgov}.
Human planners often struggle to verify compliance with complex regulations comprehensively, a challenge that can be accelerated by multimodal AI.
In this paper, we examine the ability of leading multimodal AI systems, such as Gemini, GPT, and Claude, to process planning proposals.
Central to our work is the development of GeoPlanAgent, a multimodal benchmark to evaluate two essential stages of processing planning proposals with multimodal AI: the digitisation of planning proposals and automated validation against planning regulations.
In these stages, multimodal AI needs to summarise the key idea of the planning proposal, use tools to enhance multimodal understanding, and search for relevant regulations or acts for reference.

The core components of the benchmark will include data inputs with ground truth, an output data schema, and scoring functions, potentially incorporating other tools as well.
The tools available for this paper include OpenCV for image manipulation, LoFTR for keypoint detection, and the Google Maps API for geo-coding and satellite imagery retrieval.
The paper anticipates using performance metrics such as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Text accuracy (aiming for 100\% extraction of expected text with a target of 95\% accuracy). 
    \item Date precision (94\% of dates extracted accurately with a target of 80-95\%). 
    \item Shape accuracy (90\% accurate shapes with a target of 0.8 IoU). 
    \item Location accuracy (82\% within 10\% of shape diameter for precise location, and 94\% within 50\% for general location, with respective target values).
\end{itemize}


\textbf{GeoPlanAgent:} We developed GeoMapAgent, an agentic framework compatible with leading multimodal AI systems, to improve the processing and planning of proposals.
GeoMapAgent enables multimodal AI to adaptively select tools, make complex decisions, and reflect on its mistakes to deliver reliable reviews of planning proposals end-to-end with no human intervention.

Our contributions are as follows:
1. We built a dataset to test AI agents to automatically digitise planning records. 2. We benchmark against existing models and address their weaknesses with our own agentic model.

\section{Dataset}
Our dataset comprises 270 Article~4 Direction documents paired with corresponding GeoJSON boundary data, representing the most comprehensive publicly available collection of UK planning control documents assembled to date. Spanning 52 years (1973-2025) and covering multiple local planning authorities across England, the dataset provides multimodal regulatory text, cartographic information, and extensive metadata annotations for machine learning, policy analysis, and geographic information system (GIS) applications.




\subsection{Data Collection Methodology}
We collected the dataset from the UK planning authority \cite{}. We applied a six-stage processing workflow with quality control checkpoints at each transition as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Document Acquisition:} Systematic collection from authorised source.
    \item \textbf{Quality Assessment:} Visual inspection, completeness check, authenticity verification.
    \item \textbf{Boundary Extraction:} Manual boundary map visualisation from GeoJson data.
    \item \textbf{Metadata Annotation:} Systematic coding of 13 characteristics.
    \item \textbf{Validation:} Shape matching, cross-reference with official records.
    \item \textbf{Quality Control:} Peer review, error correction, discrepancy documentation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Benchmark and Evaluation}
\subsection{Planning documents summarisation with Multi-modal Models}
\label{subsection:summarize}

Our task is to benchmark the ability of multimodal models to summarise planning proposals in PDFs.
The goal of summarisation is to extract the key idea of planning and to recognise its geometric boundary.
The challenges are in 3-fold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Summarise the planning from long PDFs. The planning PDFs are usually long (i.e., around 10 pages). Accurately summarising the key idea of planning and extracting the geometric boundaries presented in a long document is challenging for multi-modal models.
    \item Identify the human-written annotations in the planning and refine the boundaries. For example, some human annotations, such as `excluding No. 10 and No. 11,' will change the original geometric boundaries of the planning proposal. These annotations are usually hard to recognise as they are rough and small.
    \item Visualise the planning proposal on the map. This requires Multi-modal models to have tool-use ability, such as accessing the Google Map API and the geographic coordinate system.
\end{itemize}

We benchmark the leading multimodal models, including Gemini-2.5, GPT-4, and Claude-3, on this task. We use the metrics introduced in Section~\ref{section:intro} for evaluation. We plan to employ efficiency metrics, such as the token expense per planning document, to evaluate the efficiency of multi-modal models.

\section{GeoPlanAgent}
The extraction of geographic boundaries from planning documents represents a challenging intersection of computer vision, natural language understanding, and geospatial reasoning. Planning documents—such as zoning maps, environmental impact reports, and urban development plans—contain critical geographic information encoded in both visual (maps, diagrams) and textual (coordinates, landmark descriptions) formats. Automating the extraction of this information has significant implications for urban planning, environmental monitoring, and geographic information systems (GIS) applications.

Recent advances in multimodal Large Language Models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in processing both textual and visual information. However, systematic evaluation of these models on specialised geospatial tasks remains limited. GeoMapAgent addresses this gap by providing a standardised benchmark for evaluating LLM performance on geographic boundary extraction.

\subsection{Methodology}
\label{subsection:methodology}

GeoPlanAgent decomposes the boundary-extraction task into a three-agent pipeline orchestrated by multimodal LLMs. The first phase (the \emph{Reader}) is a single-shot pass that converts the raw PDF binary plus per-page OCR text into a structured \texttt{PDFInfo} record summarising every geographic clue present in the document\textemdash postcodes, OS grid references, road and place names, the printed scale, the page on which the canonical site map appears, ranked per-page metadata for multi-area documents, and a handful of administrative-region fields. The second phase (the \emph{Worker}) takes the \texttt{PDFInfo} summary together with the pre-rendered map page and registers it against Ordnance Survey basemap tiles by calling a toolkit of six operations (\Cref{tab:worker-tools}). One of those tools, \texttt{propose\_centers}, delegates to a third agent (the \emph{Locate sub-agent}) which selects one candidate map centre by calling six offline geocoder tools and inspecting the rendered map image. Two further operations (boundary segmentation and re-projection) run automatically inside the worker's positioning tools rather than as separately addressable steps. The LLM is retained for tasks that require multimodal reading: extracting structured clues from the PDF (Reader), choosing which named feature on the map best disambiguates the site (Locate), and orchestrating the registration loop with access to visual feedback panels (Worker). The full pipeline is summarised in \Cref{fig:pipeline-overview}.


\subsection{Problem Formulation}

The geographic boundary extraction task is formulated as follows:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=5pt]
    \item \textbf{Input}: A planning document PDF $D$ containing maps and/or textual geographic descriptions
    \item \textbf{Ground Truth}: A GeoJSON Feature $G_{\text{true}}$ representing the planning area boundary
    \item \textbf{Output}: A predicted GeoJSON Feature $G_{\text{pred}}$ extracted by the LLM
    \item \textbf{Objective}: Minimize spatial divergence between $G_{\text{pred}}$ and $G_{\text{true}}$ as measured by IoU, Precision, Recall, and F1 metrics
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prompt Engineering Strategy}

Each of the two agent phases is governed by a dedicated system prompt with structured output enforcement. The Reader prompt is field-by-field, telling the model how to populate each \texttt{PDFInfo} entry: which page to nominate as the canonical site map (the page that most clearly shows one drawn boundary at a useful scale, ranked first), how to disambiguate site addresses from council letterhead addresses, how to collapse house-number ranges into compact form, how to extract directional modifiers (``north of \dots'') in a normalised form, and crucially which fields to trust the OCR text block for over re-reading the rasterised image. The Worker prompt is workflow-driven: it specifies the canonical positioning loop (\texttt{propose\_centers} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{match\_at} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{commit\_match} $\rightarrow$ (\texttt{verify\_position}) $\rightarrow$ structured \texttt{BoundaryOutcome}), defines the decision rules over the multi-axis reward returned by \texttt{match\_at} (commit immediately on a strong match, try at least one more candidate on borderline scores, reject on overall scores below 0.40 and retry at a different centre; the prompt also explains that a \texttt{road\_name\_agreement} of 0.5 with verdict ``no OS roads within radius'' is a sparse-rural signal rather than a wrong-area signal, so the agent should not penalise it), forbids the agent from inventing coordinates not produced by \texttt{propose\_centers}, and caps the number of \texttt{match\_at} attempts per case at five. Boundary segmentation and re-projection to WGS84 are not separately addressable tools\textemdash they run automatically inside \texttt{match\_at} (mask production) and \texttt{commit\_match} (projection) so the agent cannot leave the loop with a positioned map but no polygon. Both phases use structured outputs enforced via pydantic schemas (\texttt{PDFInfo} for the Reader, \texttt{BoundaryOutcome} for the Worker), which means the model physically cannot return free text. The Worker's output validator additionally re-prompts the model whenever the submitted \texttt{BoundaryOutcome} fails its tool-call preconditions\textemdash for example, every borderline match in the 25--100 inlier band must be visually verified by \texttt{verify\_position} (with notes of at least 20 characters describing the comparison) before it can be accepted, and every district-wide fallback must include a successful \texttt{lookup\_district} call.

\subsection{Spatial Evaluation Metrics}

The framework implements four complementary spatial metrics based on geometric set operations:

\subsubsection{Intersection over Union (IoU)}

IoU measures the overall overlap quality between predicted and ground truth geometries:

\begin{equation}
\text{IoU} = \frac{\text{Area}(G_{\text{pred}} \cap G_{\text{true}})}{\text{Area}(G_{\text{pred}} \cup G_{\text{true}})}
\end{equation}

Range: $[0, 1]$, where 1 indicates perfect overlap.

\subsubsection{Precision}

Precision quantifies the proportion of the predicted area that correctly overlaps with the ground truth:

\begin{equation}
\text{Precision} = \frac{\text{Area}(G_{\text{pred}} \cap G_{\text{true}})}{\text{Area}(G_{\text{pred}})}
\end{equation}

High precision indicates minimal over-prediction (few false positives).

\subsubsection{Recall}

Recall measures the proportion of the ground truth area successfully captured by the prediction:

\begin{equation}
\text{Recall} = \frac{\text{Area}(G_{\text{pred}} \cap G_{\text{true}})}{\text{Area}(G_{\text{true}})}
\end{equation}

High recall indicates minimal under-prediction (few false negatives).

\subsubsection{F1 Score}

The F1 score provides a harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, balancing both metrics:

\begin{equation}
\text{F1} = \frac{2 \times (\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall})}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{equation}

This metric penalises models that optimise for only precision or recall.

\subsection{Pipeline Architecture}
\label{subsection:alignment-pipeline}

The pipeline is structured as four stages\textemdash reading, locating, matching, and segmenting. Reading and locating are LLM calls (the latter a sub-agent with its own geocoder toolkit); matching and segmenting are deterministic services exposed to the Worker as tools. \Cref{fig:pipeline-overview} provides a schematic overview.

\begin{figure*}[t]

  \begin{center}

    \caption{Overview of the GeoPlanAgent pipeline. Stage~1 (Reader) extracts a structured \texttt{PDFInfo} record from the PDF binary and per-page OCR text. Stage~2 (Locate sub-agent) sees the rendered map and the \texttt{PDFInfo} summary, calls six offline geocoder tools (postcode, grid\_ref, place, road, road-intersection, LA containment), and emits a single \texttt{LocatePick} carrying a centre, an uncertainty radius $\sigma$, and a confidence label. Stage~3 (Match) registers the planning-map crop against styled OS Open Zoomstack tiles using MINIMA-LoFTR feature matching followed by RANSAC affine recovery. Stage~4 (Segment) extracts the boundary mask with a LoRA-fine-tuned SAM~3 and projects it through the recovered affine into WGS84, yielding the predicted GeoJSON polygon.}
    \label{fig:pipeline-overview}
  \end{center}
  \vskip -0.2in

\paragraph{Stage 1: PDF reading.}
A single multimodal LLM call (Gemini~3~Flash~\cite{google2025gemini3} by default) is given the raw PDF binary together with per-page text obtained from a born-digital extractor (PyMuPDF) where possible and a two-step OCR cascade (macOS Vision~$\rightarrow$~PaddleOCR) where not, and asked to populate the \texttt{PDFInfo} schema. The primary OCR backend is the macOS Vision framework (Apple Neural Engine accelerated, $\approx0.5$--2\,s per page on Apple Silicon, English with language correction enabled); PaddleOCR serves as a cross-platform fallback for environments without Vision or when Vision returns empty on a particular page. The schema is enforced through structured-output decoding\textemdash the model physically cannot return free text\textemdash and exposes eighteen fields that capture both site-specific signals (postcodes, OS grid references, site address, road and place names, house-number/road pairs, parish names, visible map labels, adjacency hints, directional modifiers, and the most-likely town or city) and document-level metadata (the printed scale, total page count, the ranked list of canonical site-map pages together with one \texttt{MapPageMeta} per candidate page carrying \texttt{category}\,/\,\texttt{area\_group}\,/\,\texttt{boundary\_clarity}\,/\,\texttt{detail\_level}\,/\,\texttt{area\_signature}\,/\,\texttt{caption}, the local-authority \texttt{admin\_region}, the optional \texttt{district\_name}, and the \texttt{is\_district\_wide} flag). The page rotation needed to make a sideways-laid map upright is decided downstream by a dedicated ResNet50 classifier (\Cref{subsection:rotation-classifier}), not by the LLM Reader. The instruction explicitly tells the model to trust the OCR text block over the rasterised image whenever the two disagree on an exact string\textemdash postcodes and grid references in particular benefit from this because OCR returns the verbatim characters whereas vision-only re-reading frequently substitutes visually similar digits. The Reader is the only LLM role in the pipeline that performs work that could not equally well be done by a deterministic procedure; downstream stages neither receive the raw PDF nor invoke the LLM for any decision other than which tool to call next.

\paragraph{Stage 2: Locate sub-agent.}
The Worker's \texttt{propose\_centers} tool delegates to a separate LLM agent (the \emph{Locate sub-agent}) that produces a single \texttt{LocatePick}\textemdash a centre coordinate, an uncertainty radius $\sigma$, a confidence label (\textit{high}/\textit{med}/\textit{low}), and a short evidence string. The sub-agent is given the \texttt{PDFInfo} summary and the rendered planning-map image, and has access to six offline geocoder tools that wrap the same authoritative UK gazetteers as the earlier deterministic cascade: \texttt{postcode} resolves a UK postcode against OS Code-Point Open~\cite{os_code_point_open} (sub-metre BNG, $\sigma\approx100$\,m); \texttt{grid\_ref} parses an OS BNG letter+digit reference; \texttt{place} searches OS Open Names~\cite{os_open_names} for villages, churches, schools, named buildings, and landmarks; \texttt{road} returns the OML road centroid for a named road instance, optionally filtered to a local-authority bounding box; \texttt{intersect} computes the geometric junction of 2--3 named roads from the OML road geometry; and \texttt{la\_check} tests whether a candidate point falls inside the named local-authority polygon from OS BoundaryLine~\cite{os_boundary_line}. The sub-agent's system prompt fixes a protocol: view the map image to find labels that the reader missed, scan \texttt{PDFInfo} in order of signal specificity (site postcode $>$ grid reference $>$ house-number+road $>$ landmark $>$ road name $>$ parish $>$ LA centroid), letterhead-check non-site postcodes via \texttt{la\_check} (postcodes more than 5\,km from the named LA are dropped as council-letterhead noise), build a pool of 2--4 candidate hits, cluster them, and emit the tightest consensus. $\sigma$ is set by the picked source: $\approx 200$\,m for multi-source agreement, $300$--$500$\,m for a clean single signal such as a SITE postcode or grid reference, $800$--$1500$\,m for an ambiguous single signal such as a common road name, and the LA polygon's own radius when no better signal exists. The sub-agent is bounded to at most eight geocoder calls per case and an emergency LA-centroid pick is emitted if its loop fails entirely, so the Worker is guaranteed exactly one candidate to feed into Stage~3. After a weak match (low inliers or visual mismatch) the Worker may re-invoke \texttt{propose\_centers} with a free-text \texttt{match\_context} describing what went wrong; the sub-agent receives this together with its own prior conversation history and is prompted to pick from a different signal type on the second pass.

\paragraph{Stage 3: Sliding-window feature matching.}
The Worker calls \texttt{match\_at} with the \texttt{LocatePick} centre and $\sigma$, optionally retrying at a different centre by re-invoking \texttt{propose\_centers} with feedback (subject to a budget of five \texttt{match\_at} calls per case). The tool fetches an offline rendering of OS Open Zoomstack~\cite{os_open_zoomstack} tiles centred on the candidate (\Cref{subsection:tile-rendering}), resizes the planning-map crop so that its metres-per-pixel matches the chosen tile zoom (with $\pm15$\% perturbations to absorb DPI/scale errors), and slides the crop across the tile canvas. At each window MINIMA~\cite{ren2025minima}\textemdash a transformer-based dense-feature matcher built on the LoFTR~\cite{sun2021loftr} architecture and trained for cross-modal map-to-tile matching\textemdash returns sets of corresponding keypoints whose 2$\times$3 affine is then estimated via single-seed RANSAC~\cite{fischler1981ransac}. We default to a 4-DOF similarity transform and accept a 6-DOF full affine fallback only when it yields at least 30\% more inliers and passes a geometric-sanity check (positive determinant, $|b - (-c)| < 0.15$ on the off-diagonals to reject reflections and large shear, aspect ratio above 0.85, average scale within $[0.3, 3.0]$). A Delaunay-consistency filter~\cite{pierdicca2025delaunay}\textemdash which builds a Delaunay triangulation on the inlier keypoints and discards triangles whose source/target area ratio falls outside $[0.5, 2.0]$\textemdash is then re-applied unconditionally and only kept when at least one third of the original inliers survive. Each window receives a vanilla metric $V = n_{\text{inliers}} \cdot \text{aspect} \cdot s_{\text{scale}}$, where $s_{\text{scale}} = \max(0.1,\,1 - 2|\bar{s} - 1|)$ penalises affines whose recovered scale $\bar{s}$ deviates from the assumed value. We retain the best window per (centre, zoom) bucket and re-rank the survivors by the composite window score in \Cref{eq:composite-window}. The best-scoring window is returned to the agent together with a multi-axis reward (inlier strength, scale consistency, road-name agreement against the OS Zoomstack road layers, keypoint spatial spread) and a side-by-side visual panel of the planning map against the matched OS-tile region. The agent then decides whether to commit, try another centre, or proceed to segmentation. An additional automatic fallback: when \texttt{match\_at} returns fewer than 25 inliers or an overall score below 0.4 on the first pass, the tool transparently re-runs at $2\sigma$ on the same centre and keeps whichever attempt scores higher\textemdash this targets the rural-village failure mode where the geocoded centre is several hundred metres from the true site.

\paragraph{Stage 4: Boundary segmentation and projection.}
Stages~3 and~4 are interleaved: SAM~3~\cite{meta2025sam3} segmentation runs lazily inside the worker's \texttt{match\_at} tool on first need per page, so that by the time a candidate is committed the planning boundary mask has already been produced. The segmentation model is SAM~3 with a LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} adapter fine-tuned on hand-annotated UK planning maps using the recipe in \Cref{subsection:sam3-finetune}; the text prompt is fixed to the literal phrase ``planning boundary'' that the adapter was trained on, and the mask is cached per page across subsequent \texttt{match\_at} attempts so the segmentation cost is paid at most once per page per case. When \texttt{commit\_match} accepts a candidate it post-processes the cached mask through a three-step cleanup chain (\Cref{subsection:mask-postproc})\textemdash dropping noise components below 5\% of the largest connected component, expanding thin outlines into filled regions when the mask covers less than 10\% of its own bounding box, and morphologically closing remaining interior holes\textemdash and projects it through the committed affine into tile-canvas pixels and onward to WGS84 lat/lon via the inverse Web-Mercator transform. The resulting GeoJSON Feature is the prediction $G_{\text{pred}}$ scored by the metrics of \Cref{subsection:summarize}. For documents covering an entire administrative area (\texttt{is\_district\_wide=True} in the reader's output) the worker bypasses positioning and calls \texttt{lookup\_district}, which returns the district's offline-stored polygon from OS BoundaryLine~\cite{os_boundary_line} directly.

\subsubsection{Composite Window Score}
\label{subsubsection:scoring}

At Stage~3, every candidate centre may produce thousands of MINIMA windows; each is summarised by the vanilla metric $V$, but $V$ alone is degenerate against two failure modes that occur frequently in practice: (i) MINIMA locking onto a small cluster of high-confidence keypoints in one corner of the map, producing a high inlier count from an affine that fits only that quadrant; and (ii) the matched window landing kilometres from the geocoded anchor when MINIMA finds a coincidental match on similar urban texture far from the true site. We address both by composing $V$ with a quadrant-coverage factor $Q\in\{0,1,2,3,4\}$\textemdash the number of map quadrants containing at least one inlier keypoint\textemdash and a softmax-like distance penalty on the kilometres separating the matched centre from the geocoded anchor:
\begin{equation}
  \text{score} = V \cdot \frac{Q}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{1 + d_{\text{km}}}
  \label{eq:composite-window}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\text{km}}$ is the great-circle distance from the candidate's geocoded anchor to the predicted map centre. The composite score is what determines which window is returned to the Worker; on a 211-case overnight sweep this rescoring lifted IoU~$\geq0.8$ outcomes by five cases over the raw-metric ranking.

\subsubsection{Commit Gate}
\label{subsubsection:commit-gate}

The Worker may try several \texttt{match\_at} candidates before deciding which to commit. The \texttt{commit\_match} tool enforces two gates. The \emph{strict} gate refuses any commit whose candidate failed to produce a usable affine for every area\_group on the page (\texttt{n\_groups\_committed = 0})\textemdash this catches the worst-of-the-worst commits where MINIMA returned no inliers anywhere, forcing the worker to retry at a different centre via \texttt{propose\_centers(extra\_terms=[\dots])} rather than emit a polygon from no evidence. The \emph{smart} gate then re-ranks all stored \texttt{match\_at} attempts by a composite score that combines the inlier count with whether the predicted centre falls inside the reader-extracted local-authority polygon:
\begin{equation}
  s_{\text{commit}}(c) = n_{\text{inliers}}(c) \cdot \begin{cases} 1.0 & c \in \text{LA} \\ 0.3 & c \notin \text{LA} \end{cases}
  \label{eq:commit-score}
\end{equation}
If a stored attempt other than the one the agent asked to commit scores higher, the gate redirects the commit to that attempt. The 0.3$\times$ outside-LA penalty is small enough that a much-higher-inlier outside-LA candidate can still win\textemdash the LA polygon is itself imperfect\textemdash but large enough to dominate near-ties, which is where wrong-town homonyms typically beat the correct candidate.

\section{Implementation Details}

The agent itself is implemented on top of pydantic-ai: the Reader, Worker, and Locate sub-agent are three separate \texttt{Agent} instances that share a small set of helpers; structured outputs (\texttt{PDFInfo}, \texttt{BoundaryOutcome}, \texttt{LocatePick}) are enforced through pydantic schemas so the model cannot return free text in any phase. The Worker carries a mutable \texttt{AgentState} object across tool calls and exposes the six tools listed in \Cref{tab:worker-tools}; SAM~3 segmentation, mask post-processing and the WGS84 projection are not separately exposed but run automatically inside the positioning tools. An output validator decorated on the Worker re-prompts the model whenever the submitted \texttt{BoundaryOutcome} fails to satisfy the relevant tool-call preconditions (for example, every borderline match in the 25--100 inlier band must be visually verified before it can be accepted, and the \texttt{district\_lookup} status is rejected unless \texttt{lookup\_district} succeeded with a GeoJSON). The benchmark runner (\texttt{benchmark\_runner.py}) loads the SAM~3 and MINIMA models once, iterates over the dataset, and writes a per-case directory that contains the predicted GeoJSON, computed metrics, the agent's full message log, the recovered affine, the SAM mask, and a diagnostic visualisation of the prediction against the ground truth.

\section*{Limitations}

The dataset is UK-specific and over-represents London, Kent, and Norfolk\textemdash 67\% of cases come from just three of the 15+ local authorities sampled. Pipeline components that depend on the UK geocoder stack (Code-Point Open, OS Open Names, OS BoundaryLine, OS OpenMap Local, OS Open Zoomstack) do not transfer to other jurisdictions without re-pointing each tool at the local equivalent; the agent's framing should transfer, but we cannot empirically verify this without an analogous dataset for at least one other country.

The boundary-segmentation step assumes that the application boundary is visually drawn on the planning map. Documents whose application area is described purely by text (e.g. ``land north of 14 Manor Road'') with no overlaid boundary line fall outside the pipeline's competence\textemdash the Locate sub-agent will still produce a centre, but SAM~3 cannot segment a polygon that does not exist on the page.

The LA polygon used in the smart-commit gate (\Cref{eq:commit-score}) comes from OS BoundaryLine, which is updated semi-annually. A small number of cases\textemdash typically late-1970s and early-1980s Article~4 directions that pre-date several boundary revisions\textemdash sit just outside the current BoundaryLine polygon for their stated LA, triggering the outside-LA penalty incorrectly. We retain the case in the benchmark but the metric over-penalises the pipeline on those documents.

SAM~3 fine-tuning was performed on CUDA with bf16 mixed precision; validation (the per-fold numbers in \Cref{tab:sam3-cv}) is scored on Apple Silicon with fp16 autocast. A small amount of run-to-run nondeterminism in the MPS evaluation kernels means the cross-validation numbers are reproducible to $\pm 0.003$ rather than bit-exact. The dominant published-source bottleneck is therefore not training stochasticity but the size of the annotated training pool (211 cases), which we expect any future extension of this dataset to relax.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:impl-appendix}

This appendix expands the per-component summary in the main body. Section labels follow the data-flow order through the pipeline.

\subsection{Reader Phase}
\label{appendix:reader}

The Reader runs a single Gemini~3~Flash call (\texttt{google/gemini-3-flash-preview}) at zero temperature with the PDF binary attached as a multimodal part and the per-page OCR text concatenated as a separate text block. Per-page text is produced by a two-step OCR cascade: PyMuPDF's text layer for born-digital pages, otherwise the macOS Vision framework (Apple Neural Engine accelerated, $\approx0.5$--2\,s per page on Apple Silicon, with English and language-correction hints enabled), falling back to PaddleOCR as a cross-platform option for environments without Vision. Extraction results are cached on disk by PDF content hash so reruns are instant. The reader's prompt instructs the model to trust the OCR text block as ground truth for any exact-string field (postcodes, grid references, addresses, scale) and to fall back to vision-based reading only when the text block is missing or marked as failed for a particular page. The output schema enforces approximately twenty fields per case and includes a model-level validator that refuses any extraction in which all of \texttt{place\_names}, \texttt{road\_names}, and \texttt{visible\_map\_labels} are empty when other fields prove the document is a real planning case (the dominant silent-dropout failure mode of the LLM).

\subsection{Auto-Rotation}
\label{subsection:rotation-classifier}

Many UK planning maps\textemdash particularly historic Article~4 Direction sheets\textemdash are laid out sideways on the page. We replace the agent's earlier visual rotation decision (which destroyed several cases by rotating documents that were already upright) with a learned 4-class ResNet50~\cite{he2016resnet} classifier over the rotations $\{0°, 90°, 180°, 270°\}$. Training data is generated by applying each of the four rotations to 211 maps that have been visually confirmed upright (the same training pool as the SAM~3 fine-tune; \Cref{appendix:dataset-prep}), yielding 844 samples; we split by case using the same deterministic 5-fold assignment as the SAM~3 fine-tune (\Cref{subsection:sam3-finetune}) so that all four rotations of a given case fall in the same partition and each case is evaluated at inference by the fold that did not see it during training. At inference time we apply 4-rotation test-time augmentation\textemdash predicting on the input image plus its three CW rotations and cyclically shifting each prediction back to the original frame before averaging the softmax\textemdash and abstain (return zero rotation) whenever the top-class probability falls below 0.50. The abstention rule is what makes this reliable in practice: the failure mode is now ``no rotation'' rather than ``wrong rotation'', and the agent's positioning stage tolerates a missed rotation far better than an incorrect one.

\subsection{Locate Sub-agent}
\label{subsection:locate-cascade}

The Locate sub-agent is a pydantic-ai \texttt{Agent} (Gemini~3~Flash at zero temperature) whose role is to choose one well-justified map centre per case. Its inputs are the rendered planning-map image and a filtered \texttt{PDFInfo} summary (site address, postcodes, grid references, road and place names, parish names, admin region, visible map labels, adjacency hints, the \texttt{is\_district\_wide} flag). Its output is a single \texttt{LocatePick}\textemdash a centre coordinate, an uncertainty radius $\sigma$, a confidence label, a short evidence string, and a boolean recording whether the pick was validated against the named local-authority polygon. Six tools wrap authoritative offline UK gazetteers and an LA polygon shapefile:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{postcode(pc)} resolves a UK postcode against OS Code-Point Open~\cite{os_code_point_open} (1.6M GB postcode unit centroids at sub-metre BNG; free under OGL v3); $\sigma\approx100$\,m.
  \item \texttt{grid\_ref(gr)} parses an OS BNG letter+digit reference at any precision, returning a centre with $\sigma$ scaling from 100\,m (full eastings/northings) to 5\,km (two-letter prefix only).
  \item \texttt{place(q, la?)} queries OS Open Names~\cite{os_open_names} (2.5M GB places and roads, OGL v3) for villages, hamlets, churches, schools, named buildings, and landmarks. The optional \texttt{la} argument filters to the named local-authority bounding box to suppress wrong-region homonyms.
  \item \texttt{road(q, la?)} returns the centroid of an OML road-instance match within the optional LA bounding box, using a name-indexed cache derived from OS OpenMap Local.
  \item \texttt{intersect(a, b, c?, la?)} computes the geometric intersection of 2--3 named road LineStrings (also from OML), which pins junctions to sub-100\,m precision when the document names cross-streets.
  \item \texttt{la\_check(lat, lon, la)} tests whether a coord falls inside the named LA polygon from OS BoundaryLine~\cite{os_boundary_line} and returns the distance to the boundary (in km) when it does not; this is used both as a sanity check on a pick and as a letterhead filter on \texttt{PDFInfo.postcodes} (postcodes more than 5\,km from the named LA are dropped as likely council-letterhead noise).
\end{itemize}

The sub-agent's system prompt fixes a protocol: view the map for labels the reader missed, scan \texttt{PDFInfo} in decreasing order of signal specificity (SITE postcode $>$ grid reference $>$ house-number+road $>$ named landmark $>$ road name $>$ parish $>$ LA centroid), letterhead-check non-site postcodes via \texttt{la\_check}, build a pool of 2--4 candidate hits, cluster them, and emit the tightest consensus. $\sigma$ is set by the picked source: $\approx 200$\,m when two or more sources agree within $500$\,m, $300$--$500$\,m for a clean single signal, $800$--$1500$\,m for an ambiguous single signal, and the polygon's true radius for the LA-centroid fallback. The sub-agent is bounded to at most eight geocoder calls per case; on agent-loop failure an emergency LA-centroid pick is emitted so the Worker is guaranteed exactly one candidate to feed into Stage~3. When the Worker re-invokes \texttt{propose\_centers} after a weak match (low inliers or visual mismatch in the match panel), it can pass a free-text \texttt{match\_context} describing what went wrong; the sub-agent receives this together with its own prior conversation history (transcript carried on \texttt{AgentState.locate\_message\_history}) and is prompted to pick from a different signal type on the second pass.

\subsection{Map-to-Tile Matching with MINIMA}
\label{subsection:matching}

We use MINIMA~\cite{ren2025minima} on top of the LoFTR~\cite{sun2021loftr} backbone\textemdash a transformer-based dense matcher trained for image-to-tile correspondence under domain shift\textemdash as the workhorse of Stage~3. For each candidate centre the agent's \texttt{match\_at} tool resizes the planning map so that one map pixel corresponds to one tile pixel at the chosen Web-Mercator zoom (typically zoom 15--19; the best zoom is computed from the printed scale and the rendering DPI as $z = \log_2(C \cos\phi / m_{\text{px}})$, where $C \approx 156\,543$\,m is the equator pixel scale at zoom 0 and $\phi$ is the candidate latitude). The matcher is invoked at the chosen zoom plus its two neighbours and at $\pm15$\% scale perturbations of the chosen mpp; this absorbs both DPI metadata errors and printed scales that fall between the canonical 1:1250 / 1:2500 / 1:5000 grid points. The window stride is set to a target of $\approx100$ pixels per step (\texttt{WINDOW\_STRIDE\_TARGET=100}), with a 32-pixel floor that ensures sub-tile match positions are sampled densely on smaller tile canvases. RANSAC affine recovery uses single-seed estimation at a 10-pixel reprojection threshold; the 6-DOF fallback gates described in \Cref{subsection:alignment-pipeline} are applied unconditionally on top. The Delaunay-consistency filter is implemented over scipy's Delaunay triangulation\textemdash for each triangle we compute the source/target area ratio and discard triangles outside the band $[0.5, 2.0]$, then re-fit the affine on the surviving inliers, reverting to the original RANSAC fit whenever the filter would leave fewer than $\max(8, n_{\text{inliers}}/3)$ points.

\subsection{Tile Rendering}
\label{subsection:tile-rendering}

Tiles are rendered offline from the OS Open Zoomstack~\cite{os_open_zoomstack} GeoPackage\textemdash a $\approx750$\,MB OGL-v3 dataset that requires no API key. We use a dedicated styler that mimics the appearance of UK planning-map basemaps so that MINIMA's cross-modal gap is as small as possible: salmon-pink ($\#$FFB3B3) building footprints with grey outlines, white road fills with grey casings, light grey woodland, very light green greenspace, light blue surface water, and dark grey railways. Road widths scale with zoom (a Local Street is 1.5\,px wide at $z=17$ and proportionally wider at $z=18$/$z=19$); roads are drawn in two passes (casings first, fills on top) so that minor roads are correctly subordinated to major ones. The tile renderer projects geometry from BNG (EPSG:27700) through Web-Mercator (EPSG:3857) to per-tile pixels, with a 5\% bbox inflation to handle projection distortion at tile edges. Rendered tiles are cached to disk (\texttt{cache/os\_opendata\_tiles/}) keyed by zoom and BNG tile index; the first call for any case takes $\approx5$--20\,ms per tile and subsequent calls are sub-millisecond reads.

\subsection{SAM~3 Fine-Tuning with $k$-Fold Cross-Validation}
\label{subsection:sam3-finetune}

SAM~3~\cite{meta2025sam3} is fine-tuned with LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} adapters on a curated dataset of hand-annotated UK planning boundaries (\Cref{appendix:dataset-prep}). We use rank-16 LoRA on every $\{q, k, v, o\}$ projection and on the two MLP projections ($fc_1$, $fc_2$) of every transformer subsystem (vision encoder, text encoder, geometry encoder, DETR encoder/decoder, mask decoder)\textemdash a deliberately broad scope, since restricting LoRA to the mask decoder alone produced markedly worse instance candidates in earlier ablations even though the loss only directly supervised the semantic head. The mask embedder, the presence head, and the semantic projection layer are added as fully trainable modules on top of the LoRA adapters via PEFT's \texttt{modules\_to\_save} mechanism. Training uses a combined loss over the semantic and instance heads, mirroring the SAM~3 author recipe:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{sem}} &= 5\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}^{\alpha=0.6,\gamma=1.6} + 5\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{dice}} + r(e) \cdot 0.5\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{surf}} \nonumber \\
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{inst}} &= 5\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}^{\alpha=0.25,\gamma=2} + 5\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{dice}} + 2\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{pres}} \nonumber \\
  \mathcal{L} &= \mathcal{L}_{\text{sem}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{inst}}
  \label{eq:sam3-loss}
\end{align}
where $r(e) = \min(1, e/15)$ ramps the surface loss linearly over the first 15 epochs, the surface term penalises sigmoid prediction mass at signed-distance pixels far inside the boundary, and the instance focal+dice losses are computed on the slot selected by a Hungarian-style match (cost $= -\text{IoU} - 0.05\,\sigma(\text{cls}_i)$). The classification loss is a per-slot focal loss with a soft positive target $\sigma(\text{cls}_{\text{best}})^{0.25} \cdot \text{IoU}_{\text{best}}^{0.75}$ on the matched slot and zero elsewhere\textemdash this prevents the cls head from saturating to infinity on partially-correct masks while still anchoring slot identity across epochs. The presence BCE has target~1 everywhere because every training image contains a planning boundary; at inference the worker uses the presence head as a confidence gate. Training proceeds for up to 20 epochs with AdamW (lr $2 \times 10^{-4}$, cosine decay to 5\% of base, weight decay 0.01), batch size 1 with gradient accumulation 4, gradient clipping at 0.1, bf16 mixed precision on CUDA, and an early-stop patience of 6 epochs on semantic-head validation IoU.

We use 5-fold cross-validation; the per-case fold assignment is produced by the longest-processing-time-first bin-packing scheme described in \Cref{appendix:dataset-prep}, which respects ``stay-together'' multi-page case groups and yields roughly balanced fold sizes. Each case is held out from training in exactly one fold. At inference time the model loader keeps each fold's adapter on disk and, before the first SAM~3 forward pass of a case, the worker calls \texttt{set\_fold\_for\_case} which canonicalises the case identifier, looks it up in the fold-assignment table, and activates the LoRA from the fold that excluded that case from training. This eliminates the possibility of GT leakage when benchmarking on cases that were part of the SAM~3 training pool, and lets us report results on the entire labelled set rather than a held-out partition. To increase effective training diversity from a small annotated pool we apply a style-transfer augmentation that re-renders each ground-truth boundary in a randomly chosen style and colour\textemdash solid outline, thick or thin outline, dashed, dotted, semi-transparent fill, opaque fill, or diagonal hatching\textemdash matching the visual diversity of real planning-map boundary annotations that a model trained on a single canonical style would otherwise miss.

\subsection{Mask Post-Processing}
\label{subsection:mask-postproc}

The raw SAM~3 mask is run through a three-step cleanup chain before contour extraction. First, \texttt{keep\_dominant\_components} drops connected components below 5\% of the largest component's area (with a 100-pixel absolute floor), filtering noise blobs while preserving genuine multi-region masks where the smaller regions are 20--80\% of the largest. Second, \texttt{expand\_thin\_mask} detects masks whose foreground covers less than 10\% of their own bounding-box area (i.e.\ thin outlines rather than filled regions) and dilates them with an elliptical kernel sized to $\approx1.5$\% of the bounding box's smaller dimension, accepting the result only when the new fill stays under 50\% of the image. Third, \texttt{fill\_mask\_holes} morphologically closes small interior gaps with an ellipse kernel sized to 1\% of the smaller image dimension and then fills any remaining holes by drawing the external contours filled. The cleaned mask is then converted to GeoJSON: external contours larger than 100 pixels are simplified by Douglas--Peucker with $\varepsilon = 3$\,px, projected through the committed affine into tile-canvas pixels, and converted to WGS84 via the inverse Web-Mercator transform. Polygons are explicitly closed and emitted as a single \texttt{Polygon} feature when there is one contour or as a \texttt{MultiPolygon} when there are several.

\subsection{Training Dataset Construction}
\label{appendix:dataset-prep}

The SAM~3 fine-tuning dataset is constructed from a three-stage hand-annotation pipeline. First, each of the 270 evaluation PDFs is rendered at DPI~200 without auto-rotation (so the annotator works in the same frame as the raw PDF render) and seeded with an initial polygon obtained by projecting the ground-truth GeoJSON through a cached affine from an earlier benchmark run. Second, a single annotator reviews each pre-rendered case in a browser-based Flask UI and either accepts the seeded polygon, edits its vertices to match the boundary actually drawn on the page, or rejects the case (typically when the PDF contains no usable map). Third, the curated annotations are copied into the training dataset with deterministic 5-fold assignment by a longest-processing-time-first bin-packing scheme that preserves ``stay-together'' case groups: multi-page renders from a single source (\texttt{A108P\_p4/p5/p6}, \texttt{A4D6A\_merged\_p9/p10}, etc.) and twin sets of cases that share the same planning site are forced into the same fold. The final training pool contains 211 cases; the per-fold split is approximately 42--43 validation cases per fold.
